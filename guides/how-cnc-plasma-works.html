How the Cut Happens

1. Compressed air is forced through a tiny nozzle orifice in the torch.

2. A pilot arc sparks between the electrode (negative) and the nozzle (positive) inside the torch, ionizing the gas.

3. The ionized gas stream contacts the metal workpiece, transferring the arc from nozzle â†’ workpiece. This is the transferred arc.

4. The constricted arc superheats to ~40,000Â°F â€” roughly 7Ã— hotter than the surface of the sun â€” melting the metal instantly.

5. High-velocity gas blows the molten metal away, creating a narrow cut called the kerf.

Key Concept: Plasma cutting only works on electrically conductive materials â€” steel, aluminum, stainless, copper, brass. It cannot cut wood, plastic, glass, or stone.
Why Compressed Air?

Our Hypertherm uses shop air as the plasma gas. The oxygen in air reacts with mild steel, boosting cut speed and edge quality. Industrial systems may use pure O2, nitrogen, or argon blends for specialized materials.

What Is G-Code?

G-code is the universal language of CNC machines. It's a plain-text file of coordinates and commands that tell the machine where to move, how fast, and when to fire the torch. Example: G01 X4.500 Y2.250 F120 = move in a straight line to position (4.5, 2.25) at 120 inches/min. SheetCam writes the G-code automatically â€” you don't write it by hand.

Torch Height Control & Cut Quality
Why Height Matters

The distance between the torch tip and the metal â€” called standoff â€” directly controls cut quality. Too high = wide kerf, rounded top edges, excessive bevel. Too low = risk of collision, nozzle damage, double-arcing.

Digital Torch Height Control (DTHC)

Our Arclight includes automatic Torch Height Control that maintains the correct standoff distance during cutting â€” even on warped steel. Here's how:

1. Initial Height Sensing (IHS): Before each cut, the torch lowers until it touches the plate, establishing a Z-zero reference point.

2. Pierce Height: Torch raises to a safe pierce height (higher than cut height) and the arc fires. This protects the nozzle from molten blowback.

3. Cut Height via Arc Voltage: As the gantry moves, THC reads the arc voltage in real-time. Voltage is directly proportional to standoff distance â€” higher voltage = torch too high, lower voltage = torch too low. THC adjusts the Z-axis motor to maintain the target voltage.

The Rule: Arc voltage â†‘ = torch too far â†’ THC lowers. Arc voltage â†“ = torch too close â†’ THC raises. This happens automatically, many times per second.
